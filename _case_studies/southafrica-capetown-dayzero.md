# Sections
sections:
  - id: overview
    icon: droplets
    content: |
      Between 2015 and 2018, Cape Town experienced its **worst drought in recorded history**, driven by three consecutive years of below-average rainfall. Dam levels supplying the city fell to below **20% capacity**, prompting the city to announce a potential **&ldquo;Day Zero&rdquo;** &mdash; the date when municipal taps would be turned off and residents would collect water from 200 distribution points.

      Cape Town&rsquo;s population of **4.4 million** was asked to reduce consumption to **50 litres per person per day** (compared to a pre-drought average of around 200 litres). Through a combination of escalating water restrictions, tariff surcharges, public awareness campaigns, and enforcement, the city achieved an unprecedented **60% reduction in water demand**.

      Day Zero was ultimately averted through this demand reduction combined with the eventual return of winter rains in mid-2018. The crisis became a **global case study** in urban water vulnerability and the power of behaviour change.

  - id: timeline
    content: |
      **2015:** Below-average winter rainfall begins a multi-year drought. Dam levels start declining. **2016:** Level 2 water restrictions imposed. **2017 January:** Level 3B restrictions &mdash; 87 litres per person per day. **2017 September:** Level 4B restrictions &mdash; 50 litres per person per day. **2018 January:** &ldquo;Day Zero&rdquo; announced for 12 April 2018 if consumption is not reduced. **2018 March:** Day Zero postponed as consumption falls. **2018 June:** Winter rains arrive; dam levels begin recovering. **2018 October:** Restrictions eased to Level 5. **2019:** Dam levels return to 75% capacity.

  - id: stakeholders
    content: |
      The **City of Cape Town** led the crisis response through its Water and Sanitation Department. The **Western Cape Government** coordinated provincial emergency responses. The **National Department of Water and Sanitation** and **South African National Disaster Management Centre** provided national support.

      **World Bank** and international agencies provided technical assistance and later published extensive analysis of the crisis. The **private sector** and **agricultural sector** (which agreed to reduce allocations) played critical roles in demand reduction.

  - id: digitalisation
    content: |
      The city deployed a **real-time dashboard** showing dam levels and citywide consumption, updated daily and publicly accessible. **Smart metering** was accelerated in high-consumption suburbs, enabling identification of excessive users. A **&ldquo;Water Map&rdquo;** was published online showing household-level consumption by suburb, creating social pressure for compliance.

      Pressure management systems and **leak detection technology** were deployed to reduce non-revenue water losses in the distribution network.

  - id: hazards
    type: hazards
    exogenous: "Climate change altering rainfall patterns in the Western Cape, with models suggesting increasing drought frequency and severity. Cape Town&rsquo;s water supply was over-reliant on surface water from six major dams fed by winter rainfall."
    endogenous: "Historical under-investment in supply diversification. Population growth outpacing infrastructure investment. Delayed political response as drought severity became apparent. Inequitable pre-crisis water consumption patterns."

  - id: cost-benefit
    content: |
      **Cost:** The drought caused an estimated **R12 billion (approximately $800 million)** in economic losses across agriculture, tourism, and the hospitality sector. The city&rsquo;s emergency response and infrastructure investments cost an additional R5 billion.

      **Key Benefits:** Cape Town&rsquo;s permanent demand reduction has persisted post-crisis &mdash; consumption remains significantly below pre-drought levels. The crisis accelerated investment in **supply diversification** including groundwater, desalination, and water reuse. The experience has become a global reference for urban drought management.
