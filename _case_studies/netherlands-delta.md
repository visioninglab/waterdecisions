# Sections
sections:
  - id: overview
    icon: droplets
    content: |
      The Netherlands Delta Programme is one of the world&rsquo;s most ambitious **long-term climate adaptation strategies**. Established by law in 2012 (the Delta Act), the programme coordinates national efforts to protect the Netherlands from flooding and ensure adequate freshwater supply through **2100 and beyond**.

      **26% of the Netherlands lies below sea level** and **59% is flood-prone**. Without its system of dikes, dunes, storm surge barriers, and water management infrastructure, two-thirds of the country would be regularly flooded. The Delta Programme builds on centuries of Dutch water management, including the original **Delta Works** (completed 1997) built after the catastrophic **1953 North Sea Flood** that killed 1,836 people.

      The programme is organised around three pillars: **Water Safety** (flood protection standards), **Freshwater** (supply security during drought), and **Spatial Adaptation** (climate-proofing the built environment). It is led by the **Delta Commissioner**, an independent government-appointed official.

  - id: timeline
    content: |
      **1953:** North Sea Flood kills 1,836 people, leading to the original Delta Works. **1997:** Maeslantkering (final Delta Works barrier) completed. **2008:** Second Delta Commission recommends new long-term strategy. **2010:** Delta Commissioner appointed. **2012:** Delta Act passed, establishing the Delta Fund (&euro;1.5 billion/year). **2015:** Delta Decisions set strategic course for flood safety standards and freshwater strategy. **2017:** New flood protection standards enacted based on risk rather than probability alone. **Annual:** Delta Programme updated yearly with progress reports and adaptive adjustments.

  - id: stakeholders
    content: |
      The **Delta Commissioner** leads the programme with an independent mandate. **Rijkswaterstaat** manages national flood defence infrastructure. **21 regional water boards** (waterschappen) &mdash; some of the oldest democratic institutions in Europe &mdash; manage local flood defences and water management.

      **12 provincial governments** and **355 municipalities** integrate flood risk and climate adaptation into spatial planning. The programme involves extensive collaboration between government, knowledge institutions (including **Deltares**, the world-leading water research institute), and the private sector.

  - id: digitalisation
    content: |
      The Delta Programme leverages world-leading Dutch water expertise in digital tools:

      <div class="principle-card" style="margin-top: 0.5rem;">
        <h4>Flood Risk Modelling</h4>
        <p>Advanced probabilistic flood risk modelling underpins the new risk-based flood protection standards. Models incorporate sea level rise scenarios, river discharge projections, and economic damage assessments.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="principle-card">
        <h4>Climate Stress Testing</h4>
        <p>All municipalities are required to conduct climate stress tests using standardised digital tools that model the local impacts of extreme rainfall, heat, drought, and flooding under different climate scenarios.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="principle-card">
        <h4>National Flood Warning System</h4>
        <p>Real-time monitoring of water levels, storm surges, and river flows integrated with automated flood barrier operations (including the Maeslantkering, which closes automatically when storm surge exceeds 3 metres).</p>
      </div>

  - id: hazards
    type: hazards
    exogenous: "Accelerating sea level rise (KNMI scenarios project 0.3&ndash;1.2 m by 2100). Increasing Rhine and Meuse peak discharges from changing rainfall patterns. More intense rainfall events. Prolonged droughts affecting freshwater availability."
    endogenous: "Land subsidence in peat areas (up to 1 cm/year in some regions). Ageing flood defence infrastructure requiring continuous assessment and reinforcement. Complexity of coordinating across hundreds of implementing organisations."

  - id: cost-benefit
    content: |
      **Cost:** The Delta Fund provides **&euro;1.5 billion per year** (approximately &euro;85 per resident annually). The Flood Protection Programme alone aims to bring all primary flood defences up to the new standards by 2050 at a cost of approximately &euro;16 billion.

      **Key Benefits:** Protection of **17.8 million people** and an economy worth over &euro;800 billion annually. The new risk-based standards provide a **minimum safety level of 1:100,000 per year** for the most vulnerable areas. Freshwater strategies ensure supply during drought. The programme&rsquo;s adaptive approach reduces the risk of over- or under-investment by adjusting to new climate data every six years.

  - id: principles
    type: principles
    assessments:

        status: done
        content: "The programme&rsquo;s defining feature is its adaptive approach: strategic decisions are made now, but implementation is adjusted every six years based on updated climate science, monitoring data, and socio-economic developments. The shift from probability-based to risk-based flood standards was itself transformative."
      - principle: "Proactively Protected (P2)"
        status: done
        content: "Planning to 2100 with annual implementation budgets of &euro;1.5 billion. New risk-based flood standards provide the highest level of protection globally, with the most flood-prone areas protected to 1:100,000 annual probability."
      - principle: "Shared Responsibility (P5)"
        status: done
        content: "Multi-level governance involving national, provincial, and municipal governments plus 21 water boards. The Delta Commissioner role provides independent coordination. Costs are shared through the Delta Fund (national taxes) and water board levies (regional)."
      - principle: "Continuously Learning (P1)"
        status: done
        content: "Annual Delta Programme reports assess progress and update plans based on new knowledge. The knowledge programme, centred on Deltares and university partners, continuously generates new evidence on climate impacts and adaptation options."
      - principle: "Environmentally Integrated (P3)"
        status: done
        content: "Building on Room for the River, the programme integrates nature-based solutions alongside engineered defences. Freshwater strategies address both human and ecological water needs. Spatial adaptation policies embed climate resilience into the built environment."
      - principle: "Socially Engaged (P4)"
        status: done
        content: "Climate stress tests are required in every municipality, engaging local communities in understanding their flood and climate risks. The programme includes public communication campaigns about water safety and individual preparedness."

  - id: futures
    content: |
      The programme faces increasing pressure from accelerating sea level rise projections. A major policy question for the coming decades is whether to continue the current &ldquo;protect everything&rdquo; approach or begin planned retreat from the most vulnerable areas. The 2026 update will incorporate the latest KNMI climate scenarios.
