# Sections
sections:
  - id: overview
    icon: droplets
    content: |
      Room for the River (Ruimte voor de Rivier) was a landmark Dutch national programme that fundamentally shifted flood risk management philosophy from **&ldquo;fighting the water&rdquo; to &ldquo;accommodating the water.&rdquo;** Rather than solely raising dikes higher, the programme created more space for rivers to discharge floodwaters safely.

      The programme comprised **34 projects** along the Rhine, Meuse, Waal, and IJssel rivers, using interventions such as lowering floodplains, creating bypasses, relocating dikes further from the river, deepening summer beds, and removing obstacles. A dual objective was mandated: **flood safety and spatial quality improvement**.

      The near-flood events of **1993 and 1995**, when 250,000 people were evacuated from the river areas, triggered a fundamental rethink of the Dutch approach to living with water.

  - id: timeline
    content: |
      **1993 &amp; 1995:** Near-flood events on the Rhine and Meuse trigger policy rethink. **2000:** Spatial Planning Key Decision (PKB) process initiated. **2006:** Room for the River programme formally approved by Dutch Parliament with &euro;2.3 billion budget. **2006&ndash;2019:** Implementation of 34 projects across the river basins. **2015:** Programme largely completed ahead of schedule. **2019:** Final projects delivered and programme formally closed.

      The programme was delivered **on time and under budget**, a notable achievement for a national infrastructure programme of this scale.

  - id: stakeholders
    content: |
      The programme was led by **Rijkswaterstaat** (the Dutch Directorate-General for Public Works and Water Management) under the **Ministry of Infrastructure and Water Management**. A dedicated programme directorate managed coordination across all 34 projects.

      **17 partner organisations** including regional water boards, provincial governments, and municipalities were responsible for individual project delivery. The programme pioneered a collaborative governance model called **&ldquo;administrative agreements&rdquo;** (bestuursovereenkomsten) between national and regional partners.

  - id: digitalisation
    content: |
      The programme utilised advanced **hydraulic modelling** (WAQUA/SOBEK models) to simulate river discharge scenarios and predict the flood level reduction achieved by each intervention. **GIS-based spatial planning tools** integrated flood modelling with land use and environmental assessments.

      Post-completion, the programme areas are monitored through the national **water level monitoring network** and satellite-based subsidence monitoring.

  - id: hazards
    type: hazards
    exogenous: "Climate change increasing peak river discharges from Alpine and Central European rainfall. Sea level rise reducing the gradient for river discharge to the North Sea. The 1993 and 1995 near-flood events demonstrated that existing dike-only strategies were insufficient."
    endogenous: "Centuries of land reclamation and urbanisation in floodplains reducing natural flood storage capacity. Land subsidence in polder areas increasing flood vulnerability."

  - id: cost-benefit
    content: |
      **Cost:** **&euro;2.3 billion** programme budget, delivered under budget. Individual projects ranged from &euro;5 million to over &euro;350 million (Overdiepse Polder bypass).

      **Key Benefits:** Achieved the target of safely accommodating a Rhine discharge of **16,000 m&sup3;/s** (up from 15,000 m&sup3;/s design capacity). Created thousands of hectares of new nature areas, recreational space, and improved riverside landscapes. The dual mandate of flood safety and spatial quality has become a global model for integrated flood risk management.

        status: done

        status: done
        content: "The dual mandate of flood safety and spatial quality required extensive community participation. The Overdiepse Polder project relocated 17 farms to terp mounds, developed in close collaboration with the farming community who chose to remain in the area."
      - principle: "Shared Responsibility (P5)"
        status: done
        content: "Pioneering governance model with 17 partner organisations sharing responsibility through administrative agreements. National government funded the programme while regional bodies delivered individual projects."
      - principle: "Adaptively Transforming (P6)"
        status: done
        content: "The programme represented a fundamental transformation in Dutch flood management philosophy &mdash; from solely raising dikes higher to giving rivers more space. This paradigm shift has influenced flood management globally."
      - principle: "Continuously Learning (P1)"
        status: done
        content: "Rigorous monitoring and evaluation throughout the programme informed adaptive management. Lessons from early projects improved the delivery of later ones, and the programme&rsquo;s knowledge has been shared internationally."

  - id: futures
    content: |
      The Room for the River approach has been incorporated into the broader **Netherlands Delta Programme** for long-term climate adaptation. The concept of giving water more space continues to influence Dutch water management policy and has been adopted by other countries including the UK, Germany, and Bangladesh.
